# Design Critique Methodology: A Complete Facilitator's Guide

## Part 1: Running a Design Critique Session

### Pre-Session Preparation (Facilitator)

Before the session begins, the facilitator must establish the conditions for productive critique. Skipping preparation is the single most common reason critique sessions fail — they devolve into rambling opinion-sharing because no one defined what success looks like.

**One week before the session:**
- Confirm the presenter and the specific work to be critiqued
- Ask the presenter to identify their fidelity stage (30%, 60%, or 90%) and their top 2-3 questions for the group
- Identify the decision-maker — the person who will ultimately decide which feedback to act on (often the presenter, but sometimes a design lead or product manager)
- Send pre-read materials if applicable: user research context, business requirements, competitive analysis

**One day before the session:**
- Confirm the participant list (4-8 people is optimal; fewer than 4 limits perspective diversity, more than 8 makes equal participation impossible in a 60-minute session)
- Distribute the design artifacts with a note: "Please review these before the session. Come prepared with observations, not solutions."
- Prepare the room or virtual space: timer visible, shared document for notes, clear screen-sharing setup

**Five minutes before the session:**
- Test screen sharing and ensure all participants can see the design at full resolution
- Open the shared notes document with pre-filled sections: Statements of Meaning, Questions, Concerns, Suggestions, Action Items
- Remind yourself: your job is to manage the process, not to offer the most feedback

### Session Roles

**Presenter (1 person).** Shares the work, provides context, answers questions, and listens. The presenter should speak for no more than 5 minutes at the start and then shift to listening mode. The greatest discipline a presenter can develop is resisting the urge to defend every decision in real time. Hear the feedback, take notes, and evaluate later.

**Facilitator (1 person).** Manages time, enforces the critique framework, ensures all voices are heard, prevents bike-shedding, and redirects vague feedback. The facilitator should not also be a primary critic — the dual role creates a conflict between managing the process and contributing substantively. If the team is small, the facilitator can offer brief feedback at the end, but process management is the priority.

**Critics (2-6 people).** Provide structured feedback following the session format. Good critics ground their observations in principles, heuristics, user scenarios, or data. They distinguish between "this violates an established pattern" and "I personally would do it differently." They ask questions before offering solutions.

**Note-Taker (1 person).** Captures feedback in real time in the shared document. The note-taker records: who said it, what they said, whether it was a question/concern/suggestion, and any decisions made. Without a note-taker, 60% of critique feedback is lost within 24 hours — people remember the emotional tone of the session but not the specific observations.

### Session Timeline (60-Minute Format)

**0:00-0:05 — Facilitator Opening.** Welcome, state the critique format being used, remind the group of ground rules, and clarify the fidelity stage of the work. "Today we're reviewing Sarah's mid-fidelity flows for the onboarding redesign. We'll use the Liz Lerman process. Remember: ground your feedback in principles, not personal preference."

**0:05-0:12 — Presenter Context.** The presenter shares the work and provides essential context: the problem being solved, key constraints, user research inputs, and their top 2-3 questions for the group. The presenter should resist the urge to pre-justify every decision — let the work speak and let the critique surface what needs discussion.

**0:12-0:20 — Statements of Meaning.** Each critic shares what they found effective, interesting, or well-considered. This is not flattery — it is identification of strengths to preserve. Go around the room to ensure every person speaks. "I found the progressive disclosure of the pricing page effective because it lets users self-select their complexity level without being overwhelmed."

**0:20-0:28 — Presenter's Questions.** The presenter asks the questions they prepared. Critics respond, staying focused on the specific questions rather than pivoting to their own agenda. This phase ensures the presenter gets feedback on what they actually need help with.

**0:28-0:40 — Neutral Questions and Discussion.** Critics ask questions to understand intent: "What was your reasoning for the three-column layout on the comparison page?" "How do you envision this working on mobile widths?" Questions surface assumptions and reveal gaps in thinking without triggering defensiveness.

**0:40-0:52 — Opinions and Suggestions.** With permission, critics offer direct feedback. Each opinion must be grounded: cite a heuristic, reference user data, or describe a specific user scenario. "I'm concerned that the modal confirmation for the delete action doesn't communicate what will be lost — Nielsen's Error Prevention heuristic suggests the confirmation should name the specific items being deleted and whether the action is reversible."

**0:52-0:58 — Summary and Action Items.** The facilitator summarizes the key themes, the note-taker reads back action items, and the presenter confirms their understanding and next steps. Unresolved disagreements are flagged for user testing rather than extended debate.

**0:58-1:00 — Meta-Critique.** One minute for the group to reflect on the session itself: "Did we spend time on the right things? Should we change anything about our critique process?" This builds a continuous improvement loop for the critique practice itself.

### Ground Rules (Post These Visibly)

1. Critique the work, never the person. Say "this layout" not "your layout."
2. Ground every observation in a principle, heuristic, user scenario, or data point.
3. Ask questions before offering solutions.
4. Distinguish between "this violates a known pattern" and "I would personally do it differently."
5. The presenter listens more than they speak after the context-setting phase.
6. The decision-maker decides which feedback to act on — critique is input, not mandate.
7. If we disagree and cannot resolve it with principles, we flag it for user testing.

## Part 2: Session Formats

### Round-Robin Critique

Each critic speaks in turn for a fixed time (2-3 minutes), completing a full round before open discussion begins. Round-robin ensures equal voice — it prevents dominant personalities from consuming the session. The facilitator enforces strict time limits. After the round, open discussion can follow, but by that point every person has already contributed their primary observations.

**Best for:** Teams with uneven seniority levels, newly formed teams, or groups where one or two people tend to dominate discussion.

### Silent Critique (Write First, Then Discuss)

All critics spend 5-8 minutes writing their feedback independently — on sticky notes, in a shared document, or in Figma comments — before any verbal discussion begins. After the silent phase, feedback is shared aloud or clustered on a board. Silent critique eliminates anchoring bias (the first speaker's opinion shaping everyone else's) and produces significantly more diverse observations. Research on group decision-making consistently shows that independent ideation before group discussion yields higher-quality and more varied input.

**Best for:** Highly opinionated teams, sessions where anchoring bias is a known problem, complex designs where critics need time to think before speaking.

### Structured Template Critique

Critics fill out a standardized template during the session. The template might include sections like: Hierarchy Assessment, Consistency Check, Accessibility Flags, Content Clarity, Interaction Completeness, and Open Questions. This format ensures comprehensive coverage — nothing is missed because the template forces attention to every dimension.

**Best for:** Pre-handoff reviews, QA-oriented critiques, teams that tend to fixate on one dimension (usually aesthetics) at the expense of others.

### Gallery Walk

Designs are posted on walls (physical or virtual — Figma boards, Miro, FigJam) and participants circulate at their own pace, leaving written feedback as sticky notes or comments. After 15-20 minutes of individual review, the group gathers for a synthesis discussion. Gallery walk works exceptionally well for comparing multiple design directions because participants can see all options simultaneously and make comparative observations.

**Best for:** Comparing multiple design concepts, large teams (8+ people), situations where you want volume of feedback rather than depth, combined design sprints and critique cycles.

### Design Studio (Rapid Ideation + Critique Cycles)

Participants sketch solutions independently (5-8 minutes), present briefly (1-2 minutes each), critique each other's sketches, and then iterate in a second round incorporating what they learned from the critique. This format blends generative and evaluative thinking. It is not a pure critique format — it is a hybrid that uses critique as fuel for iteration within the same session.

**Best for:** Early-stage exploration (30% fidelity), situations where the problem space is well-defined but the solution space is wide open, team alignment on direction before any one designer takes a concept to higher fidelity.

## Part 3: Feedback Frameworks in Detail

### "I Like / I Wish / What If" (Stanford d.school)

Developed at Stanford's d.school as a simple, accessible framework for design feedback. Three categories:

**I Like:** Specific positive observations. "I like that the error messages include both what went wrong and how to fix it — that matches error prevention and recovery heuristics perfectly."

**I Wish:** Desires for change framed as wishes rather than demands. "I wish the navigation labels used task-oriented language instead of feature names — 'Create Invoice' rather than 'Invoice Module.'" The wish framing softens the critique without weakening it.

**What If:** Generative provocations that open new possibilities. "What if the dashboard defaulted to the user's most frequent workflow rather than a static home screen?" What-if statements push thinking beyond incremental improvement toward rethinking assumptions.

**Strengths:** Extremely low barrier to entry, works for non-designers, naturally separates positive from constructive feedback. **Limitations:** Can produce shallow feedback if participants are not pushed for specificity. "I like the colors" is not actionable. "I like the high-contrast color pairing on the CTA because it creates clear visual hierarchy" is.

### Rose-Thorn-Bud

**Rose (strengths):** What is working well and why. "The onboarding flow's progressive profile building — asking for only email at sign-up and deferring profile completion to contextually relevant moments — reduces initial friction."

**Thorn (pain points):** What is not working and what principle it violates. "The settings page presents 40+ options without grouping or hierarchy, violating the Recognition Rather Than Recall heuristic — users cannot scan for what they need."

**Bud (opportunities):** Potential improvements or unexplored directions. "The activity feed could become a coaching surface — surfacing tips based on the user's actual behavior patterns rather than generic onboarding content."

**Strengths:** The "bud" category encourages forward-looking, generative feedback that pure critique formats sometimes miss. **Best for:** Mid-fidelity reviews where both evaluation and ideation are valuable.

### STAR (Situation-Task-Action-Result)

Originally a behavioral interview framework, STAR is adapted for case study presentations and design portfolio reviews. The presenter structures their narrative:

**Situation:** The context and constraints. "Our enterprise dashboard had a 34% task abandonment rate on report generation, with user interviews revealing that the 12-step wizard was the primary friction point."

**Task:** The specific design challenge. "Reduce report generation abandonment by 50% without reducing the flexibility of report configuration."

**Action:** The design decisions made and their rationale. "We replaced the 12-step wizard with a three-phase flow: template selection (with smart defaults based on user role), parameter configuration (with a progressive disclosure panel), and preview with inline editing."

**Result:** The measurable outcome. "Report generation abandonment dropped from 34% to 11% in the first month. Average time-to-report decreased from 8.2 minutes to 3.1 minutes."

**Best for:** Portfolio reviews, case study presentations, stakeholder updates, design team retrospectives.

### Ladder of Feedback (Project Zero, Harvard)

Four rungs, executed in sequence:

**Clarify:** Ask questions to understand the work before evaluating it. "Can you walk me through the user flow when someone tries to change their subscription tier mid-billing-cycle?" Clarification prevents critics from evaluating a misunderstood version of the design.

**Value:** Identify what is strong, effective, or promising. "The inline editing pattern for subscription changes means users never leave their account page — that respects their context and reduces navigation overhead."

**Concern:** Raise concerns framed as questions, not declarations. "I wonder whether the inline editing affordance is discoverable for first-time users — the pencil icon is small and there's no instructional text. How might we signal editability?"

**Suggest:** Offer constructive suggestions tied to the concerns raised. "One option would be an empty-state tooltip on first visit that points to the edit affordance, or we could increase the icon size and add a subtle hover state that previews the edit mode."

**Strengths:** The sequential structure prevents premature judgment. By forcing clarification before evaluation, and valuing before concerning, the Ladder produces significantly less defensive reactions and more productive dialogue.

### Plus/Delta

The simplest binary framework:

**Plus (+):** What should be preserved. "The card-based layout for search results provides clear visual separation between items and supports scanability."

**Delta (change):** What should be changed and why. "The filter panel auto-collapses after each filter application, requiring users to reopen it for multi-filter refinement — this should stay open until the user explicitly closes it."

**Best for:** Quick desk critiques, time-constrained sessions, low-fidelity reviews where detail is premature. **Limitation:** No explicit category for generative/future-looking feedback.

## Part 4: Remote and Async Critique Protocols

### Figma Commenting Best Practices

- Pin comments to the specific element being discussed, not to empty space near it
- Start each comment with a type tag: [Question], [Concern], [Suggestion], [Praise], [Accessibility], [Edge Case]
- Reference the specific heuristic or principle: "[Concern - H4 Consistency] This button style doesn't match the primary CTA pattern established in the design system"
- Use threads for discussion — do not create separate comments for replies to the same topic
- Resolve comments after they are addressed to keep the signal-to-noise ratio high
- The presenter should batch-respond at scheduled intervals rather than responding in real-time, which fragments their design time

### Loom Video Critique Protocol

- Keep videos to 3-5 minutes maximum per feedback session
- Structure the video: 30 seconds of context ("I'm reviewing the checkout flow from the perspective of a returning customer"), then screen-recorded walkthrough with narrated observations
- Call out the timestamp when making each observation so the presenter can jump to relevant moments
- End with a summary: "My top three observations are..." followed by a priority ranking
- Upload to a shared channel where the presenter and team can view asynchronously

### Time-Zone-Spanning Workflows

For globally distributed teams where synchronous critique is impractical:

1. **Day 1:** Presenter shares work with context document (problem statement, constraints, user research inputs, specific questions) in a shared channel
2. **Day 1-2:** Critics in all time zones review independently and leave structured async feedback (Figma comments or shared document)
3. **Day 2-3:** Presenter reviews all feedback, groups it thematically, identifies conflicts and questions
4. **Day 3:** Presenter posts a synthesis: "Here's what I heard, here's what I plan to act on, here are the unresolved questions I need help with"
5. **Day 3-4:** Brief synchronous session (30 minutes) to resolve only the unresolved questions, scheduled at the least-bad time for all zones

This workflow takes longer than a single synchronous session but produces more thoughtful, less anchoring-biased feedback and respects the reality of distributed work.

## Part 5: Scoring Rubrics

### 10-Dimension Design Quality Rubric

Use this rubric for structured evaluations, design portfolio reviews, or systematic product audits. Each dimension is scored 1-5.

#### Dimension 1: Visual Hierarchy

- **1 (Poor):** No clear hierarchy. All elements compete for attention equally. Users cannot determine what to look at first or what matters most.
- **2 (Below Average):** Hierarchy exists but is inconsistent. Some pages have clear focal points; others are cluttered. Size, color, and position are used arbitrarily.
- **3 (Adequate):** Primary, secondary, and tertiary levels are distinguishable. Major CTAs are visually prominent. Minor inconsistencies exist but do not impede task completion.
- **4 (Strong):** Clear, consistent hierarchy across all screens. Typography scale, color weight, and spatial relationships work together to guide attention. Squint test passes on every screen.
- **5 (Exceptional):** Hierarchy is so well-crafted that a first-time user can identify the primary action on any screen within 2 seconds. Information density is high without feeling cluttered. Every element's visual weight is precisely calibrated to its importance.

#### Dimension 2: Consistency

- **1 (Poor):** Same actions look different across screens. Terminology varies for the same concepts. No evidence of a design system.
- **2 (Below Average):** Some patterns are reused but many one-off designs exist. Spacing, color usage, and component behavior vary without clear reason.
- **3 (Adequate):** Core patterns (buttons, forms, navigation) are consistent. Some edge cases show inconsistency. Design system exists but is not comprehensively applied.
- **4 (Strong):** Internal consistency is high. Platform conventions are respected. Design tokens enforce spacing, color, and typography consistently. Users can predict behavior based on visual cues.
- **5 (Exceptional):** Every screen feels like part of a unified system. Cross-platform consistency is maintained while respecting platform-specific conventions. Design system is comprehensive and well-maintained. Users never encounter surprising behavior from familiar-looking elements.

#### Dimension 3: Accessibility

- **1 (Poor):** Fails basic contrast ratios. No keyboard navigation support. No alt text. Color is the only information differentiator.
- **2 (Below Average):** Some contrast compliance. Partial keyboard support. Screen reader experience is broken or nonsensical.
- **3 (Adequate):** Meets WCAG AA for contrast. Keyboard navigation works for primary flows. Basic alt text present. Touch targets meet minimum 44px recommendation.
- **4 (Strong):** Full WCAG AA compliance. Logical focus order. Meaningful alt text. ARIA labels where needed. Works with major screen readers. Multiple input modalities supported.
- **5 (Exceptional):** Exceeds WCAG AA, approaching AAA where practical. Inclusive design principles evident — not just compliance but genuine consideration for diverse abilities. Motion-reduced alternatives exist. Cognitive load is managed for neurodiverse users. Accessibility is a design driver, not an afterthought.

#### Dimension 4: Learnability

- **1 (Poor):** Users cannot complete basic tasks without instruction. Interface vocabulary is system-oriented. No progressive disclosure.
- **2 (Below Average):** Core task is learnable but secondary features are opaque. Relies heavily on recall rather than recognition.
- **3 (Adequate):** Primary workflows are intuitive. Tooltips and contextual help exist for complex features. Onboarding covers essential functionality.
- **4 (Strong):** First-time users can complete core tasks with minimal or no instruction. Progressive disclosure reveals complexity at the right moment. Help is contextual and non-intrusive.
- **5 (Exceptional):** The interface teaches as users interact. Empty states are instructional. Complex features are discoverable through natural exploration. The learning curve is a gentle slope, not a wall. Users feel competent within minutes.

#### Dimension 5: Efficiency

- **1 (Poor):** Common tasks require excessive clicks, page loads, or steps. No shortcuts. Repetitive data entry.
- **2 (Below Average):** Tasks are completable but workflows feel laborious. Some unnecessary steps. Limited bulk actions.
- **3 (Adequate):** Primary workflows are reasonably efficient. Common actions are within 2-3 clicks. Basic keyboard shortcuts exist.
- **4 (Strong):** Workflows are streamlined. Smart defaults reduce input. Bulk operations exist for power users. Keyboard shortcuts cover common actions. Frequently used features are easily accessible.
- **5 (Exceptional):** The interface anticipates user needs. Auto-complete, smart suggestions, and context-aware defaults minimize effort. Power users have command palettes, keyboard shortcuts, and customizable workflows. The gap between intention and completion is minimal.

#### Dimension 6: Error Prevention and Recovery

- **1 (Poor):** No input validation. Error messages are cryptic or absent. No undo. Destructive actions have no confirmation.
- **2 (Below Average):** Some validation exists but errors still occur frequently. Error messages exist but lack guidance on resolution. Undo is partial.
- **3 (Adequate):** Common errors are prevented through validation and constraints. Error messages explain the problem. Undo works for most actions. Destructive actions require confirmation.
- **4 (Strong):** Proactive error prevention through smart defaults, input masks, and contextual validation. Error messages explain both the problem and the solution. Multi-level undo. Autosave prevents data loss.
- **5 (Exceptional):** The system makes errors nearly impossible through thoughtful constraints. When errors occur, recovery is effortless. Inline validation catches issues before submission. The system learns from user patterns to prevent future errors. Grace periods on destructive actions (like Gmail's "undo send").

#### Dimension 7: Aesthetics and Emotional Design

- **1 (Poor):** Visually dated or careless. No evidence of intentional aesthetic decisions. Users perceive the product as untrustworthy.
- **2 (Below Average):** Generic appearance. Functional but uninspiring. No emotional engagement. Indistinguishable from a default template.
- **3 (Adequate):** Clean, professional appearance. Appropriate color usage. Typography is readable. The design does not detract from functionality.
- **4 (Strong):** Visually distinctive and polished. Consistent aesthetic language. Color, typography, and illustration work together to create a cohesive brand experience. Micro-interactions add delight without impeding function.
- **5 (Exceptional):** The aesthetic creates genuine emotional connection. Visual design actively supports the brand promise. Details reveal care — thoughtful empty states, personality in error messages, craft in transitions. Users describe the product as "beautiful" unprompted.

#### Dimension 8: Content Clarity

- **1 (Poor):** Jargon-heavy. Labels are ambiguous. Instructions are missing or unhelpful. Users cannot understand what the interface is asking them to do.
- **2 (Below Average):** Some labels are clear but others use system terminology. Help text exists but is poorly written. Error messages are technical.
- **3 (Adequate):** Labels and instructions use user-facing language. Content is accurate but sometimes verbose. Key actions are clearly described.
- **4 (Strong):** All content is user-centered. Microcopy is concise and helpful. Labels are action-oriented. Help text appears at the point of need. Voice and tone are consistent.
- **5 (Exceptional):** Content is a first-class design element. Every word earns its place. Microcopy reduces cognitive load. The voice conveys brand personality while remaining clear. Content strategy is evident — information is layered, scannable, and contextual. Users never wonder "what does this mean?"

#### Dimension 9: Responsive Adaptation

- **1 (Poor):** Breaks on non-standard viewport sizes. No mobile consideration. Content overflows or becomes unreadable.
- **2 (Below Average):** Basic responsiveness but significant usability issues at certain breakpoints. Touch targets are too small on mobile. Key features are hidden or missing.
- **3 (Adequate):** Works across major device classes (desktop, tablet, phone). Core functionality is maintained. Some layout issues at uncommon breakpoints.
- **4 (Strong):** Thoughtful adaptation at every breakpoint. Navigation patterns shift appropriately (hamburger on mobile, expanded on desktop). Touch-friendly on touch devices, precision-optimized on desktop. Content priority shifts based on context.
- **5 (Exceptional):** Each device form factor gets a purpose-built experience, not just a reflowed layout. Mobile leverages device capabilities (camera, GPS, haptics). Desktop leverages screen real estate for power-user workflows. The experience feels native to each context.

#### Dimension 10: Emotional Impact and Trust

- **1 (Poor):** Users feel anxious, confused, or manipulated. Dark patterns are present. No trust signals. Privacy feels violated.
- **2 (Below Average):** Neutral emotional tone. Users complete tasks without distress but also without satisfaction. Trust signals are minimal.
- **3 (Adequate):** Users feel reasonably confident. Basic trust indicators (secure checkout, privacy policy) are present. No manipulative patterns.
- **4 (Strong):** The experience builds trust through transparency, predictability, and respect for user autonomy. Social proof, security indicators, and clear data practices are well-integrated. Users feel respected.
- **5 (Exceptional):** Users become advocates because the experience consistently demonstrates respect, transparency, and care. The product under-promises and over-delivers. Trust is built through actions (easy cancellation, honest communication, graceful error handling), not just signals. Users feel the product is on their side.

## Part 6: Anti-Patterns Deep Dive

### Design by Committee

**Symptom:** Every stakeholder's feedback becomes a mandatory requirement. The design becomes a patchwork of compromises that satisfies no one's vision and violates its own internal logic.

**Root cause:** No single decision-maker was identified, or the decision-maker lacks the confidence or authority to prioritize conflicting feedback.

**Solution:** Before the critique, publicly identify the decision-maker: "All feedback is valued and will be heard. Sarah is the decision-maker for this design. She will evaluate all input and determine what to act on." The decision-maker should explain their reasoning when they choose not to act on specific feedback — transparency prevents resentment.

### Vague Feedback

**Symptom:** "Make it pop." "It feels cluttered." "I don't love this." "Can we make it more modern?" These statements contain a real signal — there is something the person is reacting to — but the signal is unactionable.

**Root cause:** The critic lacks the vocabulary to articulate their observation, or the critique culture does not demand specificity.

**Solution:** When vague feedback is offered, the facilitator's job is to probe for specificity: "When you say it feels cluttered, can you point to the specific area? What elements are competing for your attention? Which element do you think should be the primary focus?" Over time, requiring specificity trains participants to think before they speak and to ground reactions in observable properties.

### HiPPO (Highest Paid Person's Opinion)

**Symptom:** When the VP of Product says "I think the navigation should be on the left," every designer in the room nods and the design changes regardless of the evidence.

**Root cause:** Power dynamics override design judgment. Junior team members self-censor to avoid contradicting authority.

**Solution:** Multiple structural interventions work. First, have the most senior person speak last in round-robin formats. Second, use silent critique so everyone commits their observations independently before any discussion. Third, frame the critique around principles rather than preferences: "Let's evaluate this against our usability heuristics" rather than "What do you all think?" When feedback is grounded in shared principles, seniority becomes less relevant than evidence.

### Bike-Shedding (Parkinson's Law of Triviality)

**Symptom:** The team spends 25 minutes debating the exact shade of blue for a secondary button while the checkout flow has a fundamental architecture problem no one mentioned.

**Root cause:** Trivial decisions are easy to have opinions about. Fundamental design decisions require deeper thinking that people avoid in group settings.

**Solution:** The facilitator must time-box topics and explicitly manage the discussion agenda. Start with the highest-impact decisions: "We have 50 minutes. Let's spend the first 30 on information architecture and flow, the next 15 on interaction patterns, and the last 5 on visual details." When bike-shedding begins, the facilitator intervenes: "This is a good observation — let's capture it in the notes and return to the flow architecture question, which has higher impact."

### Critique Avoidance

**Symptom:** The team does not do critiques at all, or critique sessions are superficial — everyone says "looks great" and the meeting ends in 15 minutes.

**Root cause:** Psychological safety is insufficient. Designers fear judgment, or past critiques were handled poorly (personal attacks, public humiliation, feedback that felt arbitrary).

**Solution:** Building a healthy critique culture is a long-term investment. Start with low-stakes formats: desk critique with trusted peers, then small-group sessions with structured frameworks that constrain feedback to be constructive, then gradually expand. Celebrate when someone raises a difficult observation: "That's a great catch — exactly the kind of feedback that saves us from shipping a problem." The facilitator must intervene immediately if feedback becomes personal or disrespectful, demonstrating that the space is genuinely safe.

### Solutioning Too Early

**Symptom:** Instead of identifying what is wrong and why, critics immediately jump to solutions: "You should use a dropdown instead of radio buttons." The proposed solution may or may not be better — but the problem was never articulated.

**Root cause:** Solution-mode is more comfortable and feels more productive than problem-identification mode.

**Solution:** Enforce a two-phase structure: first identify the problem and ground it in a principle, then propose solutions. "I notice the seven radio buttons are causing the form to feel lengthy, which may increase abandonment [problem grounded in efficiency heuristic]. Options to consider might include a dropdown, a segmented control, or a two-step progressive disclosure pattern [solutions]." The Ladder of Feedback framework naturally enforces this by requiring clarification and concern before suggestion.
