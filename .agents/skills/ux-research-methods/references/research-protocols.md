# UX Research Protocols: Comprehensive Guide

## Usability Testing Script Template (Moderator Guide)

### Pre-Session Preparation

Confirm all equipment functions correctly at least 30 minutes before the session begins. Verify screen recording software captures both the participant's screen and audio. Test the prototype or product on the exact device the participant will use. Prepare printed copies of the task scenarios, consent forms, and any questionnaires. Set the environment to a neutral state—clear browser history, close unrelated tabs, reset the prototype to its starting screen.

### Moderator Script Structure

**Welcome and Introduction (5 minutes)**

Use the following script framework, adapting the bracketed sections to the specific study:

> "Thank you for taking the time to participate today. My name is [moderator name], and I will be guiding the session. We are evaluating [product/feature name], not evaluating you. There are no right or wrong answers or actions. If something is confusing, that is valuable information for us. Please think aloud as you work through the tasks—tell me what you are looking at, what you are thinking, and what you expect to happen. The session will last approximately [duration]. Do you have any questions before we begin?"

**Warm-Up Questions (3–5 minutes)**

Open with broad contextual questions to establish the participant's background without leading toward specific product features. Example warm-up questions:

- "Describe a typical day when you need to [relevant activity]."
- "What tools or methods do you currently use for [relevant task]?"
- "How would you rate your comfort level with [relevant technology domain]?"

**Task Scenarios (30–45 minutes)**

Write each task scenario as a realistic goal the participant would naturally pursue. Avoid embedding navigation instructions or feature names in the scenario text. Structure each task block as follows:

1. **Scenario context**: A brief narrative establishing why the participant would perform this action.
2. **Task prompt**: The specific goal stated in the participant's language, not system terminology.
3. **Success criteria**: Define privately what constitutes task completion (do not share with the participant).
4. **Observation prompts**: Pre-written follow-up probes such as "What did you expect to happen there?" or "Tell me more about what you are looking for."
5. **Maximum time allocation**: Set a ceiling for each task to maintain session pacing.

Deliver tasks one at a time. Read the scenario aloud and provide a printed card with the same text. After each task, administer the Single Ease Question (SEQ): "Overall, how easy or difficult was this task?" on a 7-point scale.

**Post-Task Interview (5–10 minutes)**

Ask comparative and reflective questions after all tasks are complete:

- "Which task felt the most challenging, and why?"
- "Was there anything that worked particularly well?"
- "If you could change one thing about this experience, what would it be?"
- "How does this compare to [competitor or current tool]?"

**Wrap-Up (2 minutes)**

Thank the participant, explain next steps, process the incentive, and confirm whether the participant is willing to be contacted for future studies.

### Moderator Behavior Guidelines

Maintain a neutral demeanor throughout the session. Do not react to errors with surprise, sympathy, or correction. Use the "echo technique"—repeat the participant's last few words as a question to encourage elaboration without introducing bias. When a participant asks "Should I click this?" respond with "What would you do if I were not here?" Never say "good job" or "that's correct" after a task, as this signals evaluation of the participant.

## User Interview Discussion Guide Template

### Research Phase: Exploratory / Generative

Focus on understanding the problem space, user goals, and unmet needs. Use open-ended questions that invite storytelling.

- "Walk me through the last time you [relevant experience]. Start from the very beginning."
- "What was the hardest part about that experience?"
- "What were you trying to accomplish, and what got in the way?"
- "Describe the ideal outcome. What would success look like?"
- "Who else is involved when you [activity]? How do you coordinate?"

### Research Phase: Evaluative / Validation

Focus on reactions to specific concepts, designs, or prototypes.

- "What is your first impression of this [concept/screen]?"
- "What do you think this [element] does?"
- "How would you use this to [achieve goal from earlier discussion]?"
- "What is missing from this that you would need?"
- "On a scale of 1 to 10, how likely are you to use this? What would move that number higher?"

### Research Phase: Post-Launch / Continuous Discovery

Focus on real usage patterns, satisfaction, and evolving needs.

- "How has your workflow changed since you started using [product]?"
- "What features do you use most frequently, and why?"
- "Have you found any workarounds for things the product does not support?"
- "What would make you stop using this product?"
- "What do you wish the product did that it does not do today?"

### Interview Technique Reminders

Follow the "funnel" structure: begin with broad contextual questions, narrow toward specific experiences, then probe for detail. Use the "critical incident technique" to anchor discussions in real events rather than hypotheticals. Silence is a tool—allow 5–7 seconds of quiet after a response before probing further, as participants often elaborate unprompted.

## Contextual Inquiry Protocol

### The Master-Apprentice Model

Contextual inquiry positions the researcher as an apprentice learning from the participant (the master) in the participant's own environment. The researcher observes the participant performing real work and interrupts minimally to ask clarifying questions.

### Four Principles of Contextual Inquiry

1. **Context**: Conduct the inquiry in the participant's actual work environment, not in a lab or conference room. The physical and digital context reveals tacit knowledge that interviews alone cannot surface.
2. **Partnership**: Establish a collaborative dynamic. The participant leads the activity; the researcher follows and probes. Avoid taking over or suggesting improvements during the session.
3. **Interpretation**: Share emerging interpretations with the participant in real time. Say "It seems like you are doing X because of Y—is that right?" This allows immediate correction of misunderstandings.
4. **Focus**: Maintain a predefined research focus to avoid being overwhelmed by the richness of the environment. Define 3–5 specific aspects of the work practice to attend to.

### Session Structure

**Orientation (10–15 minutes)**: Explain the purpose and method. Emphasize that the participant should work as naturally as possible. Position yourself where you can observe the screen and physical workspace without obstructing the participant.

**Observation Phase (60–90 minutes)**: Watch the participant perform their work. Take detailed notes on actions, artifacts, communication, environmental factors, and interruptions. Ask questions at natural breakpoints: "Why did you switch to that tool just now?" or "What triggered that decision?"

**Retrospective Summary (15–20 minutes)**: At the end of the session, walk through your key observations with the participant. Share your interpretation models and ask the participant to validate, correct, or elaborate.

### Data Capture

Use a combination of handwritten field notes, photographs of the workspace (with permission), and audio recording. Video recording is ideal but may alter participant behavior in sensitive environments. Create an affinity note for each distinct observation, capturing the participant's action, the context, and the inferred motivation.

## Diary Study Setup Guide

### Study Design

Define the study duration based on the frequency of the target behavior. For daily activities, a 7–14 day study is typically sufficient. For infrequent behaviors (such as quarterly financial planning), extend to 4–8 weeks. Recruit 15–25 participants to account for dropout, which typically ranges from 20–40%.

### Tools

Select a diary tool that matches the participant's device preferences and technical comfort. Common options include:

- **dscout**: Purpose-built for diary studies with multimedia capture and structured prompts.
- **Indeemo**: Video-first diary tool with mobile-native experience.
- **Google Forms / Typeform**: Lightweight and accessible but limited in multimedia capture.
- **Messaging apps (WhatsApp, Slack)**: Low-friction for short text and photo entries; requires manual data organization.

### Prompt Design

Write prompts that are specific enough to generate useful data but open enough to capture unexpected behaviors. Structure prompts in three layers:

1. **Signal prompt**: Triggered by a specific event. Example: "The next time you compare prices between two products, open the diary and tell us about it."
2. **Scheduled prompt**: Delivered at fixed intervals. Example: "At the end of each day, describe the most frustrating technology interaction you had today."
3. **Reflection prompt**: Delivered mid-study and at study end. Example: "Looking back at your entries this week, what patterns do you notice in your behavior?"

### Cadence

Limit entries to 1–2 per day for daily studies. Excessive prompting causes fatigue and increases dropout. Send reminder notifications at times aligned with the target behavior (e.g., evening prompts for end-of-day reflections).

### Incentive Structure

Use a tiered incentive model to sustain engagement:

- **Base payment**: A guaranteed amount for enrollment and minimum participation (e.g., completing at least 70% of entries).
- **Per-entry bonus**: A small additional payment for each completed entry, encouraging consistency.
- **Completion bonus**: A larger payment upon completing all entries and the exit interview.

Example for a 14-day study in the US market: $50 base + $3 per entry + $30 completion bonus = approximately $120–$125 total.

## Card Sorting and Tree Testing Protocol

### Card Sorting

**Open Card Sort**: Provide participants with 40–80 cards representing content items or features. Instruct participants to group the cards into categories that make sense to them and to label each category. Recruit 15–30 participants for statistical reliability. Use tools such as Optimal Workshop (OptimalSort) or UserZoom.

**Closed Card Sort**: Provide the same cards plus a fixed set of predefined categories. Instruct participants to place each card into the most appropriate category. This method validates an existing information architecture rather than generating a new one.

**Hybrid Card Sort**: Provide predefined categories but allow participants to create additional categories as needed.

### Statistical Analysis for Card Sorting

- **Similarity Matrix**: Calculate the percentage of participants who grouped each pair of items together. Pairs grouped together by more than 70% of participants indicate a strong association.
- **Dendrogram (Cluster Analysis)**: Apply hierarchical cluster analysis to the similarity matrix. The dendrogram reveals natural groupings at different levels of granularity. Choose a cut point that balances depth with breadth.
- **Category Agreement Rate**: For closed sorts, calculate the percentage of participants who placed each card in the intended category. Items with less than 60% agreement may need relabeling or relocation.

### Tree Testing

Create a text-only representation of the proposed site hierarchy (the "tree"). Write task scenarios that require participants to navigate the tree to find specific content. Measure:

- **Task success rate**: Percentage of participants who found the correct location.
- **Directness**: Percentage of participants who navigated to the correct answer without backtracking.
- **Time to completion**: Average time per task, indicating navigational efficiency.

Recruit 50+ participants for tree testing to achieve statistically reliable results. Analyze paths taken to identify common wrong turns, which reveal structural or labeling problems.

## A/B Testing Protocol

### Hypothesis Formation

Structure every A/B test hypothesis in the format: "If we [change], then [metric] will [improve/decrease] by [expected magnitude], because [rationale]." Example: "If we reduce the checkout form from 5 fields to 3 fields, then checkout completion rate will increase by 8–12%, because form length is the primary friction point identified in usability testing."

### Sample Size Calculation

Before launching the test, calculate the minimum sample size using these inputs:

- **Baseline conversion rate**: The current metric value for the control.
- **Minimum detectable effect (MDE)**: The smallest improvement that would justify implementation.
- **Statistical significance level (alpha)**: Typically 0.05 (95% confidence).
- **Statistical power (1 - beta)**: Typically 0.80 (80% power).

Use an online calculator (Evan Miller, Optimizely) or the formula for a two-proportion z-test. For a baseline conversion rate of 5% and an MDE of 1 percentage point (20% relative lift), the required sample size is approximately 3,100 per variant.

### Running the Test

Randomly assign users to control and treatment groups. Ensure randomization is truly random—avoid time-based splitting or geographic splitting unless stratified. Run the test for a minimum of one full business cycle (typically 7 days) to account for day-of-week effects, even if the required sample size is reached sooner. Do not peek at results before the predetermined sample size is reached, as this inflates false positive rates.

### Statistical Significance

Calculate the test statistic and p-value using a two-proportion z-test or chi-squared test. If p < 0.05, the result is statistically significant. Report the confidence interval for the effect size, not just the p-value. Complement frequentist analysis with Bayesian probability of one variant being better than the other, as this is more intuitive for stakeholders.

### Avoiding Common Pitfalls

- **Multiple comparisons**: Tracking many secondary metrics inflates the chance of false positives. Apply Bonferroni correction or designate a single primary metric in advance.
- **Simpson's paradox**: Segment results by key dimensions (device type, user tenure) to verify the overall result is not an artifact of shifting segment proportions.
- **Novelty and primacy effects**: New designs may show initial lifts that fade. Monitor the metric for 2–4 weeks after the test concludes to confirm durability.

## Survey Design Best Practices

### Question Types

- **Closed-ended (single select)**: Use for demographic questions and forced-choice preferences. Ensure options are mutually exclusive and collectively exhaustive.
- **Closed-ended (multi-select)**: Use when participants may legitimately select multiple options. Include "Other (please specify)" to capture unanticipated responses.
- **Likert scale**: Use 5-point or 7-point scales for attitudinal measures. Label all points (not just endpoints) to reduce interpretation variability. A 7-point scale provides greater sensitivity but increases cognitive load.
- **Semantic differential**: Use bipolar adjective pairs (e.g., "Easy ___ Difficult") for quick attitudinal assessment.
- **Open-ended**: Use sparingly—typically 1–2 open questions per survey. Place them at the end to avoid fatiguing participants before quantitative items.
- **Matrix questions**: Group related Likert items into a matrix to reduce visual clutter, but limit to 5–7 rows to prevent straight-lining (selecting the same response for every row).

### Scale Design

Maintain consistent scale direction throughout the survey (e.g., always "Strongly Disagree" on the left). Avoid double-barreled questions ("How satisfied are you with the speed and accuracy of the search?"). Include reverse-coded items to detect acquiescence bias, then recode before analysis.

### Order Effects

Place sensitive or demographic questions at the end. Randomize the order of non-dependent questions to mitigate primacy and recency effects. Present general questions before specific ones to avoid priming (the "funnel" approach).

### Pilot Testing

Administer the survey to 5–10 colleagues or proxy participants before launch. Measure completion time (target under 10 minutes for unsolicited surveys, under 20 minutes for incentivized panels). Review open-ended responses for misinterpretation. Check for ceiling and floor effects in scale items. Run a soft launch with the first 50 responses and verify data quality before scaling up.

## Remote vs. In-Person Protocol Adaptations

### Usability Testing

| Factor | In-Person | Remote Moderated | Remote Unmoderated |
|--------|-----------|------------------|---------------------|
| Think-aloud quality | High (natural) | Moderate (requires prompting) | Low (often absent) |
| Setup complexity | High (lab, equipment) | Moderate (video call, screen share) | Low (tool handles it) |
| Participant pool | Limited by geography | National / international | Largest possible pool |
| Session duration | 60–90 min typical | 45–60 min (fatigue higher) | 15–30 min max |
| Observation of context | Full (body language, environment) | Partial (face, screen) | Screen only |

For remote moderated sessions, send technical setup instructions 24 hours in advance. Conduct a 5-minute tech check at the start of the session. Use a backup communication channel (phone number) in case of connectivity issues. For remote unmoderated testing, write ultra-clear task instructions since there is no moderator to clarify.

### Interviews

Remote interviews work well for most research questions. Use video calls with cameras on to maintain rapport. Record the session (with consent) and use transcription services for analysis. In-person interviews remain superior for sensitive topics, highly emotional subject matter, and when observing physical artifacts or environments is essential.

### Card Sorting and Tree Testing

These methods are inherently well-suited to remote unmoderated execution via dedicated tools (Optimal Workshop, Maze). In-person card sorting with physical cards is reserved for situations requiring real-time observation of the sorting rationale.

## Participant Screening Questionnaire Template

Design the screener to identify qualified participants without revealing the selection criteria (which would allow respondents to game their answers).

### Standard Screening Questions

1. **Demographics**: Age range, gender, location (as relevant to the study).
2. **Technology usage**: "Which of the following devices do you use at least once a week?" (List devices relevant to the product.)
3. **Category behavior**: "How often do you [target behavior]?" (Use frequency ranges.)
4. **Brand usage**: "Which of the following [product category] have you used in the past 6 months?" (Include target product, competitors, and decoy brands.)
5. **Decision role**: "When your household purchases [product category], what is your role?" (Options: primary decision maker, shared decision maker, no involvement.)
6. **Professional disqualifiers**: "Do you or any members of your household work in any of the following industries?" (Exclude market research, advertising, the product's industry to avoid biased participants.)
7. **Recent participation**: "Have you participated in a research study in the past [3–6] months?" (Exclude frequent research participants to avoid professional respondents.)

### Screener Logic

Use branching logic to terminate unqualified respondents early without revealing which answer disqualified them. End with a generic "Thank you, we will be in touch if your profile matches our study requirements" message for both qualified and unqualified respondents.

## Consent Form Requirements and Templates

### Required Elements

Every research consent form must include:

1. **Study purpose**: A clear, jargon-free description of what the study is investigating.
2. **Procedures**: What the participant will be asked to do and how long it will take.
3. **Recording**: Explicit disclosure of all recording methods (audio, video, screen capture). State who will have access to recordings and how long they will be retained.
4. **Data usage**: How the data will be used, who will see it, and whether it will be shared outside the research team.
5. **Confidentiality**: Assurances that the participant's identity will not be associated with published findings.
6. **Voluntary participation**: A statement that participation is voluntary and the participant may withdraw at any time without penalty.
7. **Risks and benefits**: An honest assessment of any risks (minimal for most UX research) and benefits (typically limited to the incentive).
8. **Contact information**: Name and contact details for the research lead and, if applicable, the IRB or ethics review body.
9. **Signature and date**: Space for the participant's signature (physical or electronic) and the date.

### Template Snippet

> I, [participant name], agree to participate in a research study conducted by [organization]. I understand that the session will be [audio/video] recorded and that the recordings will be used solely for internal research purposes. I understand that my participation is voluntary and that I may withdraw at any time. I have read and understood this consent form and agree to participate.

### GDPR and Privacy Considerations

For participants in the EU/EEA, include a lawful basis for processing (typically consent under Article 6(1)(a) of the GDPR), a statement of data subject rights (access, rectification, erasure, portability), the identity and contact details of the data controller, and the data retention period. Consult legal counsel for jurisdiction-specific requirements.

## Session Recording and Note-Taking Best Practices

### Recording Setup

Use dedicated recording software (OBS Studio, Lookback, or the platform's built-in recording) rather than relying solely on video conferencing recordings, which may compress quality. Record the participant's screen, audio, and (for moderated sessions) the participant's face in a picture-in-picture layout. Always have a backup recording running on a separate device.

### Note-Taking Framework

Assign a dedicated note-taker for each moderated session. The note-taker should capture:

- **Timestamp**: Approximate time in the session for later reference to the recording.
- **Observation**: What the participant did or said (factual, not interpretive).
- **Participant quote**: Verbatim quotes that are particularly revealing.
- **Interpretation**: The note-taker's inference about what the observation means (clearly separated from the factual observation).
- **Severity / Priority flag**: A preliminary rating of the issue's impact (critical, major, minor, cosmetic).

Use a structured template in a spreadsheet or dedicated tool (Dovetail, EnjoyHQ, Condens) to maintain consistency across sessions.

### Post-Session Debrief

Conduct a 10-minute debrief immediately after each session while observations are fresh. The moderator and note-taker should align on key findings, surprising moments, and any emergent hypotheses. Record the debrief notes in a shared document.

## Incentive and Compensation Guidelines by Market

### General Principles

Compensate participants fairly for their time and expertise. Incentives should be high enough to ensure adequate recruitment and show-up rates but not so high as to coerce participation or attract participants motivated solely by the payment. Offer the incentive regardless of whether the participant completes all tasks, to maintain the voluntary nature of the study.

### Market-Specific Guidelines (approximate ranges for a 60-minute session)

| Market | Consumer | Professional / B2B | Executive / C-Suite |
|--------|----------|---------------------|---------------------|
| United States | $75–$125 | $150–$300 | $300–$500+ |
| United Kingdom | 50–80 GBP | 100–200 GBP | 200–350 GBP |
| Western Europe | 60–100 EUR | 120–250 EUR | 250–400 EUR |
| India | 1,500–3,000 INR | 3,000–6,000 INR | 6,000–12,000 INR |
| Brazil | 150–300 BRL | 300–600 BRL | 600–1,200 BRL |
| Australia | 100–150 AUD | 200–350 AUD | 350–600 AUD |

### Payment Methods

Offer payment methods accessible to the participant population. Common options include digital gift cards (Amazon, Visa prepaid), bank transfers, PayPal, and charitable donations in the participant's name. For international studies, services like Tremendous or Rybbon simplify cross-border incentive distribution. Avoid cash for remote studies due to logistical challenges.

## Accessibility Considerations in Research Sessions

### Recruiting Participants with Disabilities

Actively recruit participants who use assistive technologies (screen readers, switch devices, voice control, magnification) when the product must be accessible. Partner with disability advocacy organizations for recruitment. In the screener, ask about assistive technology use rather than disability status—the relevant variable is the interaction method, not the diagnosis.

### Session Accommodations

- **Visual impairments**: Ensure the testing environment is compatible with screen readers (JAWS, NVDA, VoiceOver). Provide all materials in accessible digital formats. Allow extra time for task completion.
- **Hearing impairments**: Provide real-time captioning (CART) or sign language interpretation. For remote sessions, enable live captions in the video conferencing tool.
- **Motor impairments**: Allow participants to use their own assistive devices and input methods. Do not require mouse-only interactions in the test tasks.
- **Cognitive considerations**: Simplify task instructions. Offer written and verbal instructions simultaneously. Allow frequent breaks.

### Inclusive Research Environment

Ensure the physical lab or interview space is wheelchair accessible, including doorways, restrooms, and the testing station. For remote sessions, verify that the chosen video conferencing and screen-sharing tools are compatible with assistive technologies. Test the tools with a screen reader before the first accessible session.

## Research Ops: Scheduling, Recruiting, Compensation, and Panel Management

### Scheduling

Use a dedicated scheduling tool (Calendly, Ethnio, or Great Question) that allows participants to self-schedule from available time slots. Send a confirmation email immediately upon booking, a reminder 24 hours before the session, and a final reminder 1 hour before. Overbook sessions by 10–15% to account for no-shows. Maintain a waitlist of backup participants who can fill last-minute cancellations.

### Recruiting

**Internal panels**: Maintain a database of users who have opted in to research participation. Segment the panel by demographics, product usage, and recency of last participation. Rotate participants to avoid over-reliance on a small group of "super-responders."

**External recruiting**: Use services such as User Interviews, Respondent, or Prolific for hard-to-reach populations. For B2B research, leverage LinkedIn Recruiter, industry associations, and the sales team's network. For general consumer research, social media advertising with a screener link is cost-effective.

### Compensation Workflow

Establish a standardized compensation process:

1. The moderator confirms session completion and marks the participant as "completed" in the tracking system.
2. The research operations coordinator initiates payment within 24–48 hours of session completion.
3. The coordinator sends a confirmation message to the participant once payment is processed.
4. The finance team receives a monthly reconciliation report of all research incentives paid.

### Panel Management

Maintain a research CRM (Great Question, Rally, or a custom Airtable/Notion database) with the following fields for each panelist:

- Contact information and communication preferences.
- Demographic and firmographic data.
- Products or features used.
- Participation history: study name, date, incentive paid, method (interview, usability test, survey).
- Consent status and data retention expiry date.
- Opt-out / do-not-contact flag.

Enforce a minimum interval between study invitations (typically 30–90 days) to prevent participant fatigue and professional respondent behavior. Purge records of participants who have not engaged in 12–18 months, in compliance with data retention policies. Send periodic engagement communications (e.g., a newsletter summarizing how participant feedback influenced product changes) to maintain panel health and demonstrate the value of participation.

### Research Operations Maturity Model

Research operations typically evolves through three stages:

1. **Ad hoc**: Individual researchers handle their own recruiting, scheduling, and compensation. No centralized tools or processes.
2. **Emerging**: A part-time research operations role coordinates logistics across the team. Standardized tools and templates are adopted.
3. **Scaled**: A dedicated research operations function manages recruiting panels, compensation budgets, tool procurement, compliance, and knowledge management. Research velocity increases significantly.

Assess the organization's current maturity level and invest in the next stage's capabilities to unlock greater research throughput without proportionally increasing headcount.
